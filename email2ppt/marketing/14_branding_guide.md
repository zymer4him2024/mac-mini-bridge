# Branding Guide — [PRODUCT NAME]

**Written for someone new to branding.** Every decision in this guide is explained with the reasoning behind it, so you can change anything you disagree with — once you understand *why* the default exists.

> **The most important thing to know upfront:** Your brand is not your logo. Your brand is the impression a customer has after they've used your product. The logo, color, and font are tools that *signal* the impression you want to create. They matter, but they're 20% of the work. The other 80% is whether your product, copy, and customer interactions match what those visuals promise.
>
> Don't spend three months on a logo before you've sold anything. Spend one focused week now, then iterate as you learn.

---

## Part 1 — What "branding" actually means at this stage

For a small SaaS at your stage, branding is **five concrete decisions** plus one piece of discipline:

1. **A name** people can say, spell, and remember.
2. **A color palette** of 1 dominant + 1 accent + 2 neutrals.
3. **A typography choice** of 1 font (or 2 if you're disciplined).
4. **A voice and tone** rule set so your writing sounds like one person, not a committee.
5. **A small visual motif** that repeats across everything — a shape, a treatment, a way you frame screenshots.

The discipline: **once you decide, use the same 5 things everywhere for at least 6 months.** Most first-time founders constantly tweak. Tweaking erases the only branding asset you actually have at this stage — *consistency*.

What you do NOT need yet:
- A 40-page brand guidelines document.
- A custom illustration system.
- A custom typeface.
- An animated logo.
- A "brand video."
- A trademark filing in 50 countries.
- A debate about whether to be "playful" or "professional."

These come later, if at all. They are signals of brand maturity, not requirements for one.

## Part 2 — The Name

### Working name: "Pipelane"

I suggested *Pipelane* because:
- It's two short, easy syllables: PIPE-lane.
- It hints at the product without spelling it out (the sales *pipeline*, the lead *lane*).
- It's a real-feeling word — not a made-up techy spelling like "Pyplane."
- It works in conversation: *"We use Pipelane for inbound."*

But you should pressure-test it before committing. Run this 4-question check:

> 1. **Can I get the .com?** Check `pipelane.com` right now. If unavailable, can I get it for under $5,000? If no, move on.
> 2. **Is the trademark clean?** Search `https://tmsearch.uspto.gov/` for "Pipelane" — is anyone using it for software? If yes, move on.
> 3. **Does it survive the phone test?** Read aloud to a friend: *"It's spelled P-I-P-E-L-A-N-E."* Did they spell it back correctly? If no, move on.
> 4. **Does it survive the search test?** Google "Pipelane." Are the first 10 results crowded with other brands? If yes, you'll struggle for SEO. Consider moving on.

If it passes all four, register the domain TODAY. Domains get squatted fast once a name is shared in public.

### Three alternatives to keep in your back pocket

If Pipelane fails any test, try these in order:

| Name | Why it might work | Why it might not |
|---|---|---|
| **Threadly** | Clean, evokes email threads. Modern, friendly. | "Threadly" might already be taken in adjacent spaces. |
| **Curbside** | Memorable, ownable, slightly unexpected for B2B. | Doesn't hint at the product at all — needs strong copy to do the work. |
| **Inboxlift** | Self-explanatory, B2B-appropriate. | A bit literal. Risks being forgotten next to more distinct names. |

### What makes a good B2B SaaS name

- **2–3 syllables.** Long names get truncated mentally.
- **No numbers or hyphens.** Look unprofessional, hurt searchability.
- **Easy to spell from hearing it.** If you have to say "T as in tango," the name is wrong.
- **Available as .com.** Anything else is a tax you pay forever.
- **Trademark-clear in your category.** A lawsuit at year three is worse than a renaming at month one.

### What does NOT matter

- Whether the name "describes" the product. *Stripe* doesn't describe payments. *Slack* doesn't describe messaging. Strong brands fill names with meaning over time, not the other way around.
- Whether your friends like it. A name is a vehicle for a brand you don't have yet — they're judging the empty vehicle. Trust them on the *shortlist*, not the *winner*.

## Part 3 — The Logo

### The honest truth about logos at your stage

**Almost no one's buying decision is influenced by your logo.** Customers care about whether the product solves their problem. A clean, professional, forgettable logo does 95% of the job a famous logo does. So don't optimize for the famous version yet.

Three budget tiers:

### Tier 1 — Free / DIY ($0, 2–4 hours of your time)

This is what I recommend for the first 90 days unless you have specific reason not to.

1. **Pick a simple wordmark.** Your name in a strong font is a logo. *Stripe*'s logo for years was just the word "Stripe." So is *Linear*. So is *Notion* now (with a small mark next to the wordmark).
2. **Use Figma (free) or Canva (free)** to set the wordmark in your chosen font (see Part 5). Use your accent color. That's it.
3. **Optional:** Add a single shape mark beside the wordmark — a circle, square, or simple geometric form in your accent color. Keep it simple enough that you could draw it on a napkin.

A wordmark in *Inter* or *Söhne* or *SF Pro* with a clean accent color will look more professional than 70% of B2B SaaS logos out there. The bar is lower than people think.

### Tier 2 — Cheap professional ($150–$500, ~1 week)

When you have your first paying customer and want a logo that looks intentional, not improvised:

- **99designs** (~$300–$500): Run a contest, get 30+ submissions, pick a winner. Quality varies.
- **Looka or Looka Pro** ($20–$200): AI logo generator. Output is decent but generic.
- **Fiverr Pro** ($100–$300): Hire a vetted designer for a single logo. Read reviews carefully.

**What to ask for:**
- A wordmark version (just the name)
- A symbol/mark version (just the icon)
- A combined version (mark + wordmark)
- Files in SVG, PNG (transparent), and a PDF style guide
- Black, white, and color variants of each

That's a logo *system*, not just a logo. It's what you actually need.

### Tier 3 — Real designer ($2,000–$8,000, 3–6 weeks)

Wait until you have $50K+ ARR or you're raising a seed round. Anything earlier is premature optimization.

Find designers via:
- Behance, Dribbble (filter by SaaS / B2B branding)
- Recommendations from other founders in your network
- Studios like *Ramotion*, *Focus Lab*, or *RoAndCo* (these are the upper end — $20K+)

**Brief them on:**
- Your customer (sales leader, security buyer, founder)
- Your one-sentence positioning
- Three brands whose visual style you admire (and *why*)
- Three brands whose style you reject (and *why*)
- The deliverable: logo system + minimal brand guidelines (10–15 pages)

A good Tier 3 result lasts you 5+ years. A bad one is wasted money. Read their portfolio carefully.

## Part 4 — Colors

### The recommended palette (you can change it)

Following the brand guardrails from your INDEX, here's the concrete palette to lock in:

| Role | Color | Hex | When to use it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary accent** | Deep blue | `#1F4FFF` | Buttons, links, key call-to-actions, the visual motif. The single color that says "this is [Product Name]." |
| **Ink (text)** | Near-black | `#111111` | Body text, headlines on light backgrounds. Slightly softer than pure black, easier to read. |
| **Soft (secondary text)** | Mid-gray | `#6B7280` | Sub-headlines, captions, less important text. |
| **Paper** | Pure white | `#FFFFFF` | Default background. |
| **Tint** | Very pale blue | `#EEF2FF` | Subtle backgrounds for callouts and highlight boxes. Same hue family as the accent. |

### Why these specific choices

- **One bold accent, not three.** A single accent color is what your brain associates with your brand. Three accents = no brand.
- **Deep blue, not generic blue.** `#1F4FFF` is more saturated and modern than the corporate blues most B2B SaaS uses. It signals confidence without being aggressive.
- **Off-black, not pure black.** `#111111` reads softer on screens. Pure black creates harsh contrast that tires the eye.
- **Pure white background, not gray.** Apple-clean. Lets the accent color do the work.

### If you want a different feel

Three alternative palettes you could use, each evoking a different mood:

| Mood | Primary | Ink | Background |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Warm/approachable** | Coral `#FF5A45` | `#1A1A1A` | `#FFFFFF` |
| **Premium/serious** | Midnight `#0E1A2B` (use as primary background instead of accent) | `#FFFFFF` | `#FFFFFF` body, dark headers |
| **Trustworthy/established** | Forest green `#2C5F2D` | `#1A1A1A` | `#FAFAF8` (cream paper) |

Pick one and commit for at least 6 months. Brand identity comes from repetition, not variety.

### Accessibility check (do this once)

Use [https://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/](https://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/) and confirm:
- Body text on background passes **AA** (contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1).
- Large headlines pass **AA Large** (≥ 3:1).
- Your accent color, when used on text, passes AA against your background.

The recommended palette above passes. If you change colors, re-check.

## Part 5 — Typography

### Recommended: Inter (one font, two weights)

Use **Inter** for everything. It's:
- Free (open source, hosted by Google Fonts).
- Designed specifically for screens.
- Used by Stripe, Linear, Mozilla, Figma, GitHub, Vercel, and most modern B2B SaaS.
- Available in every weight you'll ever need.

Embed it in your website with one line:
```html
<link href="https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Inter:wght@400;600;700&display=swap" rel="stylesheet">
```

### The two-weight rule

Use only:
- **Inter Regular (400)** for body text and most copy.
- **Inter Bold (700)** for headlines and emphasis.

Optionally:
- **Inter SemiBold (600)** for sub-headlines, only if you have a clear reason.

Do not use Light, Thin, ExtraBold, or Italic. Disciplined typography looks designed; expressive typography looks amateur.

### Type sizes (for the website)

| Element | Size | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Hero headline | 56–72px | Bold (700) |
| Section headline | 32–40px | Bold (700) |
| Sub-headline | 18–24px | Regular (400), gray |
| Body text | 16–18px | Regular (400) |
| Caption / metadata | 12–14px | Regular (400), gray |

### If you want a more "designed" feel

Pair **Inter** for body with one of:
- **Söhne** (paid, $200/year — what Anthropic uses) for headlines
- **General Sans** (free) for headlines
- **Instrument Serif** (free) for hero headlines if you want a more editorial feel

Two fonts is the maximum. Three is a tell that you're new at this.

## Part 6 — Voice and tone

This is where most early-stage brands lose distinctness. Voice is what makes your blog post sound like *you* and not the same generic SaaS copy as everyone else.

### Your voice in three rules

**Rule 1 — Confident, calm, plain-spoken.**
You know what you're doing. You don't need to shout to prove it.

**Rule 2 — No marketing jargon.**
Banned words and phrases:
- *Synergy, leverage, best-of-breed, paradigm, robust, seamless, frictionless*
- *AI-powered, AI-driven, intelligent, smart* (just say what it does)
- *Solutions* (you sell a product, not "solutions")
- *Enterprise-grade* (let security teams decide that)
- *Revolutionary, game-changing, disruptive*
- *Cutting-edge, next-generation, world-class*

**Rule 3 — One idea per sentence.**
Compound sentences hide weak thinking. Break them apart.

### Tone shifts by audience (same voice)

Voice is the consistent personality. Tone shifts based on who you're talking to. Same person, different context.

| Audience | Tone shift |
|---|---|
| **SMB sales leader** | Confident, slightly direct. They want answers fast. |
| **Enterprise security buyer** | More precise, more measured. Use specific terms (DPA, SOC 2, GDPR Article 44). |
| **Founder peer** | Conversational, personal. First person ("I built this because…"). |
| **Existing customer** | Warm, helpful, no selling. They've already bought. |

### Examples

❌ **Generic:**
> "Our AI-powered platform leverages cutting-edge machine learning to deliver world-class lead intelligence and seamless CRM synchronization."

✅ **Your voice:**
> "[Product Name] reads every inbound email, summarizes the lead in 200 words, and updates your CRM. Setup takes 90 seconds."

❌ **Generic:**
> "We are excited to announce our revolutionary new feature that will transform the way you do sales."

✅ **Your voice:**
> "Two-way Salesforce sync is live today. If you've been waiting for it, you can turn it on now."

The pattern: replace claims with specifics.

## Part 7 — The visual motif

A motif is the small, recurring element that makes your brand feel cohesive across every touchpoint. Most first-time founders skip this and their brand feels disconnected.

### Pick ONE of these and use it everywhere

| Motif | What it looks like | Where to use it |
|---|---|---|
| **The accent edge** | A 6px-wide accent-color rectangle at the top-left of cards, headers, and slide titles. | Website cards, slide layouts, email signatures, PDF report covers. |
| **The accent dot** | A small filled circle in your accent color, used as a separator or marker. | Section headers, navigation, breadcrumbs. |
| **The corner cut** | One corner of every card is cut at 45° in your accent color. | Pricing cards, feature blocks. |
| **The number badge** | Numbered steps shown as a small filled circle with white text inside. | How-it-works sections, onboarding screens. |

I used the **accent edge** in the one-pager you have, and the **number badge** in the pitch decks. Pick one and use it consistently across everything from now on.

### Why motifs work

When a customer sees an asset of yours for the seventh time, the motif is what their brain recognizes. Not the logo. Not the color alone. The motif. It's the cheapest brand-recognition tool you can deploy and the one most often skipped.

## Part 8 — The minimum brand asset kit

Build these in your first month. Skip everything else.

| Asset | Purpose | Tool |
|---|---|---|
| **Logo (wordmark)** | Universal use. Email signatures, footer, favicon. | Figma |
| **Logo (mark only)** | Square avatars (LinkedIn, GitHub, X, app icon). | Figma |
| **Favicon (32×32)** | Browser tab. Often overlooked, instantly tells visitors you're real. | Export from logo |
| **OG image (1200×630)** | The image that appears when your homepage is shared on LinkedIn/Slack/Twitter. | Figma template |
| **Email signature** | Used in every outbound email. | Plain HTML or Mailcastle |
| **Slide template** | One layout system for every customer-facing deck. | Already built — your three pitch decks share this. |
| **One-pager template** | The leave-behind PDF. | Already built — your sales sheet docx. |
| **Letterhead** | For formal proposals or DPAs. | Word/Pages template |

That's 8 assets. Most first-time founders skip 5 of them and over-invest in the other 3. Build all 8 and you're ahead.

### Tools to use (all free or near-free)

- **Figma** (free) — design tool. Use it for the logo, OG image, slide template improvements.
- **Canva** (free / $13/mo Pro) — easier than Figma, fine for early templates.
- **Google Fonts** (free) — Inter and any future fonts.
- **TinyPNG** (free) — compress images so your website loads fast.
- **Coolors.co** (free) — generate and test color palettes.
- **Removebg** (free / $9/mo) — remove backgrounds from product screenshots.
- **Loom** (free / $15/mo) — record product demos for sales calls.

Total monthly cost for a complete brand toolkit: **$0–$40**. Don't spend more until you're past $10K MRR.

## Part 9 — A 7-day branding sprint

If you can dedicate one focused week to branding, here's how to use it.

### Day 1 — Decisions
- Confirm or change the name. Run the 4-question check from Part 2.
- Lock the color palette (default or alternative from Part 4).
- Lock the typography (Inter is the default).
- Pick the visual motif.

By end of day 1, the five branding decisions are made and written down.

### Day 2 — The logo
- Open Figma. Set your name in Inter Bold at 96pt in your accent color. Save.
- Try three variations: wordmark only, wordmark with a small mark, mark only.
- Export each as SVG and PNG.
- Pick a winner, sleep on it.

### Day 3 — The minimum asset kit
- Build the favicon (32×32) and the OG image (1200×630) in Figma.
- Set up your email signature.
- Add the logo to the existing one-pager and pitch decks.

### Day 4 — Voice and tone
- Read the bad/good examples in Part 6.
- Audit your existing landing page copy. Rewrite anything that fails the voice rules.
- Write 3 sample LinkedIn posts in your voice and have a friend (or me) read them blind. Did they sound like one person?

### Day 5 — Website refresh
- Apply the new visuals to your website. Same copy, new colors and font and motif.
- Compare before/after on mobile and desktop. Take screenshots.

### Day 6 — Test in market
- Send 3 cold emails using your new brand and voice.
- Post 1 LinkedIn post in your voice.
- Update LinkedIn / X profile photos and banners with the new brand.

### Day 7 — Document and lock
- Write a one-page brand cheat sheet (template below). Pin it where you'll see it weekly.
- Commit: no changes for 6 months unless you discover a customer-facing reason to change.

## Part 11 — Common first-time-founder mistakes

Save yourself months by skipping these:

1. **Chasing perfection on the logo.** It will look "wrong" for the first 6 months no matter what. That's normal. Familiarity makes it look right over time.
2. **Changing the brand every quarter.** Repetition is the only branding tool that compounds. Tweaking erases it.
3. **Hiring an agency too early.** $20K on branding before $10K MRR is a sign you're avoiding the real work (selling).
4. **Picking a name based on .com availability alone.** A name that's hard to say or remember is worse than a name with a hyphenated domain.
5. **Building a 40-page brand guidelines doc.** Nobody reads them. The one-page cheat sheet does the job.
6. **Treating branding as separate from product.** The way the product feels (speed, clarity, voice in error messages) is part of your brand. Don't outsource it.
7. **Using stock photography of people in headsets.** Customers can smell stock. Use real product screenshots, real customer logos (when you have them), real photos of you and your team. Or no people at all.
8. **Letting marketing copy and product copy diverge.** The button labels in your app should sound like your landing page. Audit this once a quarter.

## Part 12 — When to revisit this guide

Come back here:
- Before launch — to make sure everything's locked.
- At 10 paying customers — review what's working in customer interactions.
- At 100 customers — likely time for the Tier 3 designer pass.
- Before any rebrand discussion — to remind yourself of the cost of changing.
- When you make your first hire who has to use the brand — they need this document.

You don't need to revisit it weekly. The whole point of locking decisions is that you stop spending mental energy on them.
