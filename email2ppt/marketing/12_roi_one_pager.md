> *Defaults are conservative averages from our pilot customers. Replace with your actual numbers if you know them.*

### Calculation

```
Monthly leads dropped         = leads/mo × drop_rate
Monthly deals lost            = leads_dropped × conversion_rate
Monthly pipeline lost ($)     = deals_lost × avg_deal_value
Monthly ROI ($)               = pipeline_lost − product_cost
Annual ROI ($)                = monthly_ROI × 12
```

### Worked example (using defaults)

```
Leads/mo                      = 80
Drop rate                     = 25%
Leads dropped/mo              = 80 × 0.25 = 20
Conversion rate               = 8%
Deals lost/mo                 = 20 × 0.08 = 1.6
Avg deal value                = $7,500
Pipeline recovered/mo         = 1.6 × $7,500 = $12,000
[PRODUCT NAME] cost/mo        = $[X]
Net monthly value             = $12,000 − $[X]
Annual recovered pipeline     = $144,000
```

> The default scenario recovers **$144,000 in annual pipeline** for the cost of one mid-sized SaaS subscription.

## Why we don't quote a "10x ROI" headline

Because most ROI calculators in B2B SaaS are dishonest. They count the value of a 1% conversion lift on revenue that wasn't there to begin with. They claim time savings that no one tracks.

Our framework is more conservative because we want it to survive a CFO's pen.

We count **only**:

1. **Leads not dropped.** Measurable. The tracker shows it.
2. **Time not spent re-summarizing.** Optional input — most teams don't bother to count this, even though it's real.

We do **not** count:

- "Conversion rate uplift from better data quality" (real, but unmeasurable in your first year).
- "Brand value of professional Monday reports" (real, but immeasurable).
- "Reduced rep churn from less inbox grunt work" (real, but a 12-month signal, not a procurement signal).

If we wanted to inflate the number, we could. We're choosing the version that survives.

## The hidden costs of NOT doing this

For prospects who say "we'll just have an SDR do it manually":

| Cost | What it actually costs |
|---|---|
| One full-time SDR | $65,000–$95,000 fully loaded |
| Their attention on triage | ~30% of their week, or roughly the cost of a part-time SDR |
| Friday-afternoon dropoff | Triage quality falls hardest on the worst day of the week |
| Onboarding the next SDR | Every rotation loses institutional knowledge of "what was that lead about?" |

[PRODUCT NAME] is not a substitute for an SDR. It's the difference between asking your SDR to do *both* outbound prospecting *and* inbound triage versus asking them to do one of those things well.

## How to present this on a call

1. **Do the discovery first.** Don't open the calculator until you have their actual lead volume and deal size. Their numbers are more persuasive than yours.
2. **Use their drop rate, not the default.** Ask: "Be honest — what percentage of inbound do you think gets dropped?" The number they say out loud is the most powerful number in the conversation.
3. **Compute live.** Open this doc, fill in their numbers, do the math while they watch. The cognitive labor of the calculation is part of the close.
4. **End with the worst-case line.** "Even if you cut every assumption in half, you still recover [$X] a year. The downside is a tool you cancel after 30 days. The upside is a closed deal that wouldn't have happened."

---

## A note on payback period

Most pilots produce a measurable result inside the first **2–3 weeks**. By the time procurement signs the annual contract, the customer has typically already seen one recovered lead pay for the year.

This is not a "12-month payback" tool. If a prospect is told it is, the math is being padded somewhere.
