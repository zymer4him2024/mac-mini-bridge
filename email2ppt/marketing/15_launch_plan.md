# Product Launch Plan — [PRODUCT NAME]

**Written for someone new to launching a B2B product.** This is not a marketing-theory document. It's a 12-week playbook with specific things to do each week.

> **The most important thing to know upfront:** A B2B SaaS launch is not a single event. It's a series of moments that build on each other. The companies you admire that "launched on Product Hunt" actually had 6 months of quiet groundwork before that day. Plan for the slow work. The "launch day" is the smallest part of the plan.
>
> If you only read one thing in this document, read **Part 2 — The three launches**. That framing changes everything.

## Part 2 — The three launches

A B2B SaaS doesn't have one launch. It has three.

### Launch 1 — Private beta (with design partners)

**What it is.** You give 5–10 hand-picked customers free or deeply discounted access in exchange for feedback, case studies, and quotes. They use the product in real conditions. You learn what's actually broken.

**What it isn't.** A press moment. Nobody outside those 5–10 people should hear about it.

**Goals.**
- Validate the product on real inboxes and real workflows.
- Generate 2–3 case studies and quotes you can use later.
- Get word-of-mouth referrals to similar buyers.

**Time:** 6–8 weeks before public launch.

### Launch 2 — Public launch (the moment)

**What it is.** The day you go from "private beta" to "anyone can sign up." Press, social, Product Hunt, your network. The day you've been building toward.

**Goals.**
- Get 100+ signups in week 1.
- Land 1–2 press mentions in publications your target buyers read.
- Convert 5–10 of those signups into paying customers within 30 days.

**Time:** Day 0 of the public phase. Plan this carefully.

### Launch 3 — Ongoing launches

**What it is.** Every meaningful product update, customer story, or feature release is its own mini-launch. You ship a Salesforce integration → you announce it. You publish a case study → you announce it. You hit a milestone → you announce it.

**Goals.**
- Stay top of mind with prospects who weren't ready in Launch 2.
- Build a track record of momentum that investors and enterprise buyers respect.
- Compound your audience over time.

**Time:** Forever, every 2–4 weeks.

> **The mistake first-time founders make:** Treating Launch 2 as the whole launch. Pouring 90% of effort into one day. Then going dark afterward. Launch 2 is a peak; the surrounding weeks are what makes the peak meaningful.

## Part 3 — The 12-week plan

This is a recommended runway. If you're tighter on time, see **Part 7 — The minimum 4-week version**. If you have more time, the right answer is usually to spend the extra weeks on Launch 1 (private beta) — not to extend Launch 2 prep.

The plan assumes:
- You launch publicly at end of week 12.
- You're working with a small team (1–3 people).
- Bootstrap budget — $0–$2,000 in launch spend total.

Let's call today **Week 0**.

---

### Phase 1 — Foundations (Weeks 1–4)

**Goal of this phase:** Have a website, brand, and 5 signed design partners by end of week 4.

#### Week 1 — Brand and basics

- Lock branding decisions (use the [branding guide](14_branding_guide.md), Day 1 of the 7-day sprint).
- Register the domain. Set up email at the domain (`hello@`, `you@`, `support@`).
- Create LinkedIn / X / GitHub profiles for the company.
- Buy the .com if you haven't already.

**Estimated time:** 8–10 hours.

#### Week 2 — Website v1

- Build the landing page using `01_landing_page_copy.md` (sales-led version).
- Tools (pick one): Webflow ($23/mo), Framer ($15/mo), or Carrd ($19/year for the cheapest version).
- Add a single CTA: "Book a demo" or "Join the waitlist."
- Add a 90-second product video (record with Loom, embed via the share link).
- Check on mobile. Most B2B traffic is desktop, but reps Slack each other links from phones.

**Estimated time:** 10–15 hours.

#### Week 3 — Identify and reach out to design partners

A design partner is a real customer who agrees to use the product in real conditions in exchange for free/discounted access plus their feedback.

- Make a list of **30 ideal design-partner candidates**. They should be: (a) real B2B firms with sales teams of 5–25, (b) people you can directly contact, (c) likely to give you honest feedback.
- Use the cold email Sequence 1 (`03_cold_email_sequences.md`) — but adjust subject lines and replace "trial" with "design partner program."
- Send 30 emails. Aim for 5 yeses.

**Estimated time:** 15 hours (mostly research).

#### Week 4 — Onboard design partners + set up analytics

- Sign 5 design partners (a simple agreement, not a 30-page MSA).
- Get them onto the product. Watch them use it. Take notes.
- Install simple analytics: **Plausible** ($9/mo, privacy-friendly) or **Fathom** ($14/mo). Skip Google Analytics — overkill at this stage.
- Set up a simple email tool: **Beehiiv** (free tier) or **MailerLite** (free tier under 1K subs) for your future newsletter.

**Estimated time:** 15 hours.

> **End of phase 1 checklist:**
> - [ ] Domain, email, brand, logo, favicon all live
> - [ ] Landing page live
> - [ ] 5 design partners signed and using the product
> - [ ] Analytics installed
> - [ ] Email newsletter tool ready

---

### Phase 2 — Build the audience (Weeks 5–8)

**Goal of this phase:** Build 200+ followers on LinkedIn, 50+ newsletter subscribers, and at least one written case study draft.

#### Week 5 — Founder content cadence

This is where most first-time founders skimp and pay for it later. The 4 weeks of consistent content before launch is what *makes* launch day work.

- Schedule 1 LinkedIn post per week from your personal account.
- Use posts from `02_social_post_pack.md`. Start with Posts 1, 6, 7, 9.
- Reply to every comment within an hour of posting.
- Share 1 of your posts to a relevant Slack/Discord community per week (see Part 4).

**Estimated time:** 4 hours/week, ongoing.

#### Week 6 — First newsletter

- Write the first newsletter. Topic: the founding story (the dropped lead that started it). Reuse Post 6 from the social pack.
- Send to: design partners, friends, anyone who's already in your network. Aim for ~30 initial subscribers.
- Add a newsletter signup form to your website.

**Estimated time:** 3 hours.

#### Week 7 — Capture customer voice

- Interview 2–3 design partners by week's end. 30 minutes each on Zoom, recorded.
- Ask: *What was the problem before? What changed when you started using it? What surprised you? What would you tell a peer?*
- These quotes will populate your case studies, social posts, and pitch deck testimonial slides.

**Estimated time:** 5 hours.

#### Week 8 — First case study draft

- Pick the strongest design partner story.
- Draft a case study using `13_case_study_template.md`. Keep it under 700 words.
- Send to the customer for approval. Don't publish yet — save it for launch week.

**Estimated time:** 6 hours.

> **End of phase 2 checklist:**
> - [ ] 200+ LinkedIn followers (from a steady cadence)
> - [ ] 50+ newsletter subscribers
> - [ ] 1 case study drafted and approved
> - [ ] Customer quotes ready to use

---

### Phase 3 — Launch ramp (Weeks 9–12)

**Goal of this phase:** Warm up press and partners, build a waitlist, and prepare a launch-week plan that runs itself.

#### Week 9 — Press list and outreach prep

- Identify **5–10 reporters** who cover B2B SaaS, sales tools, or AI privacy (use the table in `11_press_release_template.md`).
- Read their last 3 articles each. Note what they care about.
- Don't email them yet. Just compile the list, with each reporter's email and one specific recent article you can reference.

**Estimated time:** 4 hours.

#### Week 10 — Pre-launch warming

- Email the press list a heads-up: "We're launching on [date]. Wanted to flag in case it's relevant. Happy to do an exclusive briefing 1 week before if it helps." Most won't reply. 1–2 might.
- Add a "Coming [date]" banner to your homepage. Add an "early access" signup form.
- Push the early access form to your LinkedIn audience and newsletter list. Aim for 100+ signups.

**Estimated time:** 5 hours.

#### Week 11 — Launch assets ready

- Finalize: launch tweet/post, Product Hunt assets (if launching there), launch newsletter, launch email to design partners and waitlist.
- Have a friend review every asset for typos, broken links, and unclear copy. Pay $50 for a freelance proofreader if you can.
- Schedule everything that can be scheduled. Reduce day-of stress.

**Estimated time:** 10 hours.

#### Week 12 — Launch week

See **Part 5 — The launch week playbook** for the day-by-day breakdown.

---

### Phase 4 — Post-launch (Weeks 13–16)

**Goal of this phase:** Convert launch attention into 5+ paying customers and set up the ongoing-launch rhythm.

#### Week 13 — Convert the spike

- Personally email every signup from launch week. *"Thanks for joining — what made you sign up?"* The answers tell you what's actually resonating.
- Get 50% of signups onto a 14-day trial.
- Update the case study to be public. Send it to your newsletter list.

#### Week 14 — Second case study + first feature announcement

- Draft the second case study from another design partner.
- Announce one product update (an integration, a new feature, anything) with a small dedicated post + newsletter mention. Keep the launch rhythm going.

#### Week 15 — First sales review

- Sit down and look at the numbers. What converted? What didn't? Which channel produced the best customers?
- Adjust the playbook for Q2.

#### Week 16 — Plan the next launch

- Pick the next big "launch moment" — a feature, a milestone, a partnership. Aim for 6–8 weeks out.
- Reset the runway. The pattern repeats.

> **The pattern from week 16 forward:** Every 6–8 weeks, identify a new launch moment, build toward it, ship it, learn, repeat. This is how brands compound.

## Part 4 — Channels (where to actually launch)

Here's where to spend launch energy, ranked by ROI for B2B SaaS at your stage.

### Tier 1 — Highest ROI (do these well)

#### LinkedIn (founder voice)
- **Why:** Your buyers (sales leaders, security buyers, founders) live on LinkedIn. Founder posts outperform company posts 5–10x.
- **What to post:** The 10 posts in `02_social_post_pack.md`, paced one per week pre-launch, then more frequently around launch week.
- **What to skip:** LinkedIn ads at this stage. ROI is poor without an established account.

#### Direct outreach (cold email)
- **Why:** You control the targeting. You can hit your exact ICP.
- **What to send:** The sequences in `03_cold_email_sequences.md`.
- **Volume:** 30–50 personalized emails per week. Skip mass blasts.

#### Your existing network
- **Why:** Warmest leads you'll ever have. They want you to win.
- **What to do:** Personal email or DM to every relevant person in your network 2 days before launch. Ask: *"Would you forward this to anyone you think it'd help?"*

### Tier 2 — Worth doing (medium ROI)

#### Newsletter / Substack
- **Why:** Your subscribers self-select for being interested. Highest-converting audience you'll have.
- **Cadence:** Weekly during launch ramp, biweekly steady-state.

#### Communities (Slack, Discord, Reddit)
- **Why:** Your buyers hang out where peers gather. But you must be a member, not a marketer.
- **Where:** *Pavilion* (sales leaders), *Modern Sales Pros* (Slack), *r/sales*, *r/startups*, *Indie Hackers*, *Hacker News* (Show HN, used carefully).
- **Rule:** Spend 6 weeks contributing helpfully before mentioning your product once. Otherwise you'll be banned.

#### Product Hunt
- **Why:** Useful for reach if your product is interesting to a tech-savvy audience. Mixed for B2B SaaS.
- **Honest take:** Product Hunt is great if you have a strong network and a visually polished product. It's mediocre if you don't. Don't make it the centerpiece of your launch. Make it one of several channels on launch day.
- **If you do it:** Read [https://www.producthunt.com/launch](https://www.producthunt.com/launch) carefully. Launch Tuesday or Wednesday at 12:01am PST. Have a "hunter" with a strong following submit you (politely ask one in the weeks before).

### Tier 3 — Defer or skip

#### Press releases via wire services
- **Skip.** PRWeb / Business Wire are $400+ and deliver no measurable value. Email reporters directly.

#### Paid ads (Google, LinkedIn, Twitter)
- **Defer.** ROI is poor before you have product-market fit. Spend the budget on more outbound instead.

#### Conferences and events
- **Defer.** Booths cost $5K+ and convert poorly without an established brand. Attend as a participant; don't sponsor.

#### Influencer marketing
- **Skip for B2B at this stage.** Mostly a B2C tactic.

## Part 5 — The launch week playbook

Five days, structured for momentum. Adjust dates as needed; the *sequence* matters more than the calendar.

### Sunday evening (day -1)

- Final check of every asset (links, copy, screenshots).
- Send a private "heads-up" message to design partners: *"Tomorrow we go public. Your case study is on the homepage. Thank you."*
- Set up a simple internal tracking sheet for the week.

### Monday morning (day 0) — Quiet ship

- 6:00am — Update website from "Coming [date]" to live launch state.
- 7:00am — Send launch email to: design partners, newsletter list, friends, network.
- 8:00am — Post on your personal LinkedIn. Use the founder-voice launch post (template below).
- 9:00am — Reply personally to every comment, every email reply, for the next 4 hours.

> **Launch LinkedIn post template:**
> ```
> Today we launched [PRODUCT NAME].
>
> [One sentence about the problem.]
>
> [One sentence about what you built.]
>
> [One sentence on what's specifically different.]
>
> The story behind why I built it: [link to founder-story post or newsletter].
>
> If your team is fighting [problem], try it on a real inbox: [URL].
>
> Comments below for questions.
> ```

### Tuesday (day 1) — Press push

- Email the 5–10 reporters on your list with a personalized note. Reference one of their articles. Brief them on the launch and offer a 15-minute call.
- Post Day 1 update on LinkedIn: "Yesterday we launched. Here's what happened in 24 hours." Share the early metrics.
- Submit to Product Hunt (if you're going there). Or post in r/SaaS, Indie Hackers, Show HN.

### Wednesday (day 2) — Customer voice

- Publish the case study on your blog/website. Promote on LinkedIn.
- Ask the design partner customer to repost your launch post on their LinkedIn.
- Post on LinkedIn: "Here's how [Customer] used [Product] to cut their lead drop rate by [X]." Tag the customer.

### Thursday (day 3) — Detail / depth post

- Post a deeper, slightly technical LinkedIn post: how the local AI works, or why you chose privacy-first, or what surprised you in the design partner phase.
- Send a second newsletter: "Three days in — what we've learned."

### Friday (day 4) — Reflection

- Post on LinkedIn: "Week one numbers." Be honest. Even modest numbers ("32 trial signups, 2 paying customers") read as real and earn trust.
- Personally respond to every signup that came in this week.
- Schedule next week's content. The launch *peak* is over; the *climb* is just beginning.

### Saturday/Sunday — Recover

You will be exhausted. That's expected. The most common mistake in launch week is burning out so badly that weeks 2–4 collapse. Take the weekend off.

## Part 6 — Metrics

### What to track (the short list)

| Metric | What it tells you | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Trial signups** | Top-of-funnel demand | Daily for 2 weeks; weekly thereafter |
| **Trial → paid conversion rate** | Whether the product solves the problem | Weekly |
| **Active customers** | The only metric that matters long-term | Weekly |
| **MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)** | The number that pays you | Weekly |
| **Newsletter subscribers** | Owned audience growth | Weekly |
| **LinkedIn followers** | Top-of-funnel awareness | Weekly |
| **Replies to outbound** | Whether your message resonates | Weekly |

That's 7 metrics. Pin them on a single spreadsheet. Update every Friday. Resist adding more.

### What NOT to obsess over

- **Website visitors.** A vanity metric. 1,000 visitors who don't sign up are worth less than 10 who do.
- **Social impressions.** Same problem.
- **Press mentions.** Useful for ego, weak for business. The mention that matters is the one that brings you a paying customer.
- **Product Hunt votes.** Fun, fleeting, doesn't pay rent.

### A simple goal-setting framework

For each phase of the 12-week plan, set ONE primary goal:

| Phase | Primary goal | Realistic target |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 (weeks 1–4) | Sign 5 design partners | 5 signed |
| Phase 2 (weeks 5–8) | Build owned audience | 200 LinkedIn followers, 50 newsletter subs |
| Phase 3 (weeks 9–12) | Build a waitlist | 100 early-access signups |
| Phase 4 (weeks 13–16) | Convert to paying | 5 paying customers |

Five paying customers in 16 weeks sounds modest. It's actually realistic for a B2B SaaS at this stage with disciplined execution. Anything higher is a stretch goal; anything lower means something is broken in the product or the message, and that's where to focus.

## Part 7 — The minimum 4-week version

If you're already 4 weeks from your target launch date, do these 7 things and skip everything else.

### Week 1
1. Lock branding (one day, see `14_branding_guide.md` Day 1).
2. Build a single landing page with one CTA: "Get early access" → captures email.
3. Sign 2–3 design partners from your existing network.

### Week 2
4. Get the design partners onto the product and start using it. Capture quotes.
5. Write 1 case study (rough — the customer story matters more than polish).

### Week 3
6. Email 30 reporters and 100 personal-network contacts with a "we're launching on [date]" message. Build an early-access list of 50+.

### Week 4
7. Launch. Use the 5-day playbook from Part 5, compressed.

This is a thin version of the 12-week plan. It works, but only if you've already validated demand somewhere (existing customers, prior conversations, a paying pilot). If you haven't, the 12-week plan is the right call.

## Part 8 — Common first-time-founder launch mistakes

1. **Treating launch day as the whole launch.** The pre-work matters more. The post-work matters more. Launch day is just the spike.
2. **No design partners before public launch.** Public launching to strangers without 3+ real users is launching with no proof.
3. **Going dark after launch week.** The momentum from launch dies in 7–10 days unless you're posting again.
4. **Building a waitlist of strangers, not buyers.** 1,000 random signups is worse than 50 in your ICP.
5. **Picking too many channels.** Two channels done well beat seven channels done badly. LinkedIn + cold email is enough.
6. **Skipping the founder content cadence.** A launch by a person with no audience is a tree falling in a forest.
7. **Pricing too low to be taken seriously.** Free or $9/mo signals "toy." For B2B sales tools, $50/mo per inbox is the floor for being treated as serious.
8. **Pricing too high to be tried.** $500/mo per inbox at launch when you have no logos = nobody trials. Find the middle.
9. **Letting press timing block launch.** "We're waiting on TechCrunch" — TechCrunch will not save you. Launch when you're ready and use press to amplify, not gate.
10. **Comparing yourself to a Series-B startup's launch.** They've been building audience for 3 years and have a marketing team. Run your race.
11. **Forgetting to thank the people who helped.** Design partners, beta testers, friends who shared your launch post. Send them a personal note in week 13. They'll show up for you again.
12. **Quitting if launch underperforms.** Most B2B launches "underperform" relative to founder expectations. The companies that win are the ones that keep launching every 6–8 weeks until the cumulative compound effect kicks in.

  3. _____________________________________________________________

The "what I learned about my customer" line is the most valuable. Most first-time founders skip it. Founders who consistently fill it in build products that win.

## Part 10 — When to ask for help

Some moments where bringing in external help pays off, even on a bootstrap budget:

| Moment | What to hire | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Before launch — copy proof | Freelance proofreader | $50–$150 |
| Before launch — design polish | Freelance designer for 1 day of touch-up | $300–$800 |
| Launch week — coordination | A close friend or contractor to handle inbox while you focus | Trade or $300 for the week |
| Post-launch — tax/legal cleanup | Accountant for first proper review | $500 |
| Past 10 customers — first marketing hire | Part-time fractional marketer | $1,500–$3,000/mo |

Hiring before product-market fit is usually wasted money. Hiring after launch, for specific scoped work, is usually worth it.

## Part 11 — When to revisit this plan

- **End of week 4** — Are you actually on track for design partners? If not, slow down.
- **End of week 8** — Is the audience growing? If your LinkedIn followers haven't grown 5x in 4 weeks of weekly posting, the content needs work.
- **End of week 12** — Did the launch land? What was the surprise — positive or negative?
- **End of week 16** — Are you closer to product-market fit? The number you're watching is *time-to-yes* on cold demos. If it's getting shorter, you're getting closer.

The plan is a guide, not a contract with yourself. The point is to have a plan you can deviate from with intention — not no plan at all.
