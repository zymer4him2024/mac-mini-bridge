# Case Study Template — [PRODUCT NAME]

A fillable case study structure. Pasted as a standalone file for production use; the same template is referenced inside `11_press_release_template.md`.

> **When to write:** After a customer's third successful month. Earlier and the result is anecdotal; later and they've forgotten the "before" state.

---

## Title format

**How [Customer Company] cut its inbound lead drop rate from [X]% to [Y]% in [Z] weeks**

> Make the title a number-driven outcome statement. "How [Customer] uses [PRODUCT NAME]" is a weak title. The title above is the title that gets clicked and read.

---

## At-a-glance box (top of page)

```
Customer:        [Company Name]
Industry:        [Industry]
Team size:       [N people]
Use case:        Inbound lead automation on shared sales@ inbox
Time to value:   [X] days
Headline result: [Quantitative outcome — one sentence]
```

---

## Section 1 — The Challenge

**Paragraph 1 — Who they are.**
One paragraph on the customer's business, the market they sell into, and what a great quarter looks like for their sales team. Establishes who the prospect is recognizing themselves against.

**Paragraph 2 — What was broken.**
Specific, quantified pain. Replace generic phrases with numbers: not *"our pipeline was messy"* but *"our SDR was spending 14 hours a week triaging inbound, and we were still losing one in four leads to follow-up gaps."*

**Paragraph 3 — What it was costing them.**
Use a real dollar number if the customer is comfortable. "An estimated $X in pipeline lost per quarter, plus the recruiter cost of two SDR backfills in twelve months."

---

## Section 2 — Why They Chose [PRODUCT NAME]

A single tight paragraph. Cover three things:

- What they evaluated.
- What they ruled out, and why.
- What tipped the decision toward [PRODUCT NAME].

Be specific. *"We looked at HubSpot Inbox but it required us to migrate off Salesforce"* is a real reason a prospect can act on. *"We loved the team"* is filler that doesn't help anyone.

---

## Section 3 — How It Worked

**Lead paragraph — the deployment.**
What they actually rolled out, in what order. Names of inboxes connected. Whether they used SaaS or on-premise topology. Whether they ran it alongside their existing CRM or replaced anything.

**Then 3–4 milestone bullets.** The only place in the case study where bullets are appropriate, because deployment really is a list:

- Day 1: connected sales@ inbox via OAuth, took 90 seconds.
- Day 4: first batch of 20+ summaries reviewed by sales leadership, accuracy verified.
- Day 7: first weekly auto-deck delivered to the leadership team.
- Day 30: HubSpot CRM sync activated, lead tracker became source of truth.

---

## Section 4 — Results

**Paragraph 1 — The headline outcome.**
The single most important number. Highlight it visually if the case study is being designed for the website (large pull quote, big number).

**Paragraph 2 — The second-order effect.**
Something the customer didn't expect to gain. This paragraph is what makes the case study memorable. *"We thought we'd save time. We didn't expect to stop hiring SDRs."*

**Paragraph 3 — A direct quote from the buyer.**
2–3 sentences max. Attribute by full name and title. Honest > glowing.

> Example structure:
> *"[Outcome the buyer expected]. [Outcome that surprised them]. [What they'd tell a peer who is evaluating]."*

---

## Section 5 — What's Next

A single paragraph on what the customer plans to do with [PRODUCT NAME] over the next quarter. Always include this. It signals momentum to other prospects and shows what the next stage of value looks like.

Examples: *"Roll out to two more shared inboxes."* *"Activate the on-premise topology for the European subsidiary."* *"Use the auto-deck output as the basis for QBR materials."*

---

## Section 6 — About [Customer Company]

A short company description, written by the customer if possible (or pulled verbatim from their own website). Two sentences maximum. Logo, link to their site.

---

## Production checklist

Before publishing, confirm:

- The buyer has approved every quote in writing (email is fine).
- The numbers in the at-a-glance box are sourced from the customer's own data, not your dashboard.
- A real headshot of the buyer is included — not stock photography.
- The story is under 700 words. Longer = unread.
- The PDF is downloadable without a form gate. Don't trade case study access for an email; it costs you 80% of readers.
- The web version has a clear next CTA: "Book a demo" or "Read the next story."
- The customer received a polished PDF copy 24 hours before public release as a courtesy.

---

## Common mistakes (don't do these)

- **Don't quote yourself.** Only the customer is allowed to be quoted. If you find yourself wanting to add company commentary, delete it.
- **Don't claim outcomes the customer doesn't claim on the record.** If they won't say it, you don't get to write it.
- **Don't gate the case study behind a form.** PDF download is fine. Forms tell prospects you don't trust them.
- **Don't pad with screenshots.** One product screenshot is enough; the story is the customer, not the UI.
- **Don't write more than three case studies until you've published the first one.** Build one, learn what worked, then rinse and repeat. Don't draft six in parallel.

## Format options for output

| Format | Use it when |
|---|---|
| **Webpage** | Always. Live URL is the primary asset. Indexable, shareable, updateable. |
| **PDF** | Always, generated from the webpage. For sales follow-up emails and procurement packets. |
| **One-slide PPT** | For pitch decks. The case study compressed into one slide: logo + headline metric + one-line quote. |
| **30-second video** | After your third or fourth case study. Earlier is wasted production budget. |
