# Press Release Template — [PRODUCT NAME]

A standard short-form B2B SaaS press release. Use it when you announce v1, raise a round, or sign a marquee customer. Replace every `[BRACKETED]` field. Keep the whole thing under 500 words for a launch announcement.

> **When to send:** Send launch announcements via your founder LinkedIn first; press release second. PR distribution services (PRWeb, Business Wire) deliver no value at this stage and cost $400+ per release. Skip them. Email this directly to the 5–10 reporters who actually cover your space.

# Case Study Template — [PRODUCT NAME]

A fillable structure for capturing real customer wins. Use this in `13_case_study_template.md` separately. *(Including here for proximity, since press + case studies are usually built together.)*

> **When to write:** After a customer's third successful month. Earlier than that, the result is anecdotal. Later than that, the customer has forgotten the "before" state.

---

## Title format

> **How [Customer Company] cut its inbound lead drop rate from [X]% to [Y]% in [Z] weeks**

(Make the title a number-driven outcome statement. "How [Customer] uses [PRODUCT NAME]" is a weak title; "How [Customer] cut X by Y%" is the title that gets read.)

---

## Section 1 — At-a-glance metrics box

```
Customer:        [Company Name]
Industry:        [Industry]
Team size:       [N people]
Use case:        Inbound lead automation on shared sales@ inbox
Time to value:   [X] days
Headline result: [Specific quantitative outcome]
```

## Section 2 — The challenge (3 short paragraphs)

- **Paragraph 1: Who they are.** One paragraph on the customer's business, market, and what success looks like for their sales team.
- **Paragraph 2: What was broken.** Specific, quantified pain. Not "things were inefficient" — "their SDR was spending 14 hours a week triaging inbound, and even so, they were losing 1 in 4 leads to follow-up gaps."
- **Paragraph 3: Why it mattered.** What that pain cost them. Use an actual dollar number if the customer is comfortable.

## Section 3 — Why they chose [PRODUCT NAME] (1 paragraph)

What they evaluated, what they ruled out, and why. Be specific. "We looked at HubSpot Inbox but it required us to migrate off Salesforce; [PRODUCT NAME] sat in front of our existing CRM" is a real reason. "We loved the team" is filler.

## Section 4 — How it worked (1 paragraph + 3-4 bullets)

What they actually deployed, in what order, with timelines. This is where the customer's voice shines. Use direct quotes. Bullets are appropriate here because deployment milestones are inherently a list.

## Section 5 — Results (3 short paragraphs)

- **Paragraph 1: The headline outcome.** With a number. Highlight visually.
- **Paragraph 2: A second-order effect.** Something they didn't expect. ("We thought we'd save time. We didn't expect to stop hiring SDRs.")
- **Paragraph 3: A direct quote from the buyer.** 2–3 sentences max.

## Section 6 — What's next (1 paragraph)

What the customer plans to do with [PRODUCT NAME] next quarter. Always have one of these — it shows momentum and signals to other prospects what the next stage of value looks like.

---

## Mistakes to avoid in case studies

- **Don't quote yourself.** Only the customer is allowed to be quoted. If you find yourself wanting to add a line of company commentary, delete it.
- **Don't claim outcomes the customer doesn't claim.** If they won't say it on the record, you don't get to write it.
- **Don't use stock photos.** A real headshot of the buyer outperforms any stock asset by 4x in conversion testing.
- **Don't gate the case study behind a form.** PDF download is fine; "fill out 8 fields to read this story" kills 80% of readers and tells your prospects you don't trust them.
